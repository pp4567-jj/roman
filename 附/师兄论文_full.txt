ABSTRACT
In this study, we proposed a Spiking Neural Network (SNN) based framework for
analyzing Raman spectral data. The model was trained to predict the concentration of
three
substances(Malachite
Green,
4-MBA and
Thiram)
by
analyzing
their
Surface-Enhanced Raman Scattering(SERS) spectra at various concentrations, an
average prediction accuracy exceeding 92% was achieved. As for testing Malachite
Green from real samples, the value of R┬▓ was significantly higher than several other
comparative models, that including Convolutional Neural Networks(CNN) and
Multilayer Perceptrons(MLP). Furthermore, the method was successfully applied for
quantitative detecting pesticide from real sample. This work establishes that SERS spectral
analysis using SNN models represents a feasible, efficient, and precise method for
quantitative analysis.
Keywords: Meaching learning; Spiking neural networks; SERS; Quantitative
prediction, pesticide; real sample

1.
Introduction
Since its discovery, surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy (SERS) has been a
powerful analytical tool, with the significant enhancement of the Raman signal
primarily attributed to two factors: charge transfer between the analyte and the
metallic substrate, and localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) in metallic
nanomaterials upon light excitation1-3.This capability enables SERS to push the
detection limit to the single-molecule level while maintaining its ability for facile and
rapid identification of substance concentrations or materials, leading to widespread
applications in food security analysis and various other fields4-6.Additionally, machine
learning algorithms can be integrated with SERS spectroscopy to leverage their
powerful computational capabilities for rapid identification of Raman features in each
spectrum and the construction of predictive models for swift recognition and analysis.
In recent years, deep learning technology has advanced rapidly, driving
remarkable breakthroughs in various pattern recognition tasks, including fundamental
image recognition and
segmentation, object
detection, and
speech
and text
processing7-10.Meanwhile, the integration of deep learning with Raman spectroscopy
has demonstrated significant potential in spectral identification. For example, in the
domain of convolutional neural networks (CNNs), a dense convolutional neural
network (DenseCNN) with over 40 layers was proposed to enhance feature
propagation, enabling the identification of Raman spectra from 32 liquid chemicals10.
Additionally, a model combining residual networks with one-dimensional CNNs
(1D-CNN) was developed for the analysis of common bacteria11.Furthermore, a
hybrid network integrating Savitzky-Golay convolutional smoothing with CNN was
introduced to suppress noise and extract features in SERS spectra12;In the field of
long short-term memory (LSTM) networks, Wu et al. achieved qualitative and
quantitative analysis of phthalates in extra virgin olive oil (EVOO) by integrating
SERS
with
an
LSTM
neural
network13.Lin
et
al.
rapidly
identified
dibenz(a,h)anthracene (DbA) and benzo(b)fluoranthene (BbF) in mussels using a
similar integration of SERS and LSTM networks14.

However, most current algorithms rely on empirical parameter adjustments ŌĆö
such as smoothing window width and baseline correction thresholds ŌĆö during
preprocessing, which can lead to the loss of valuable information15, 16. Moreover,
continuous activation functions such as Softmax often struggle to effectively address
the non-stationary characteristics of SERS signals17-19.
In order to address these challenges, we propose a Spiking Neural Network
(SNN) based framework. The third-generation spiking neural network (SNN) has
advanced brain-inspired networks by drawing on neurophysiological insights,
controlling neuronal activity through high/low membrane potentials, and discarding
the complex computations of traditional deep neural networks. This enables
ultra-low-power operation and event-driven processing feasible for neuromorphic
hardware. A spiking neural network consists of spiking neurons, each of which fires
an output spike only when its membrane potential is charged above a certain threshold
20-23. The generated spike is propagated into the neurons in the next layer and
increases/decreases their membrane potentials. In this manner, the communication
between neurons is performed by spikes.
The rate encoding was employed in SNN model24, 25, where Poisson-distributed
random numbers are used to sample pulses over T time intervals for encoding,
enabling direct conversion of raw spectral data into spatio-temporal pulse sequences.
Thus avoiding systematic errors introduced by traditional preprocessing methods. The
bio-inspired Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) neuron model is used to construct the
output layer26-28, and the Spike-Timing-Dependent Plasticity (STDP) mechanism
captures the temporal evolution of spectral features. To address non-differentiability
of the pulse signal, an error backpropagation mechanism based on the Surrogate
Gradient method is employed to optimize network parameters, enabling parameter
optimization while maintaining biological plausibility28, 29. The model was ultimately
tested on three types of substances for concentration analysis and selected pesticides
for real sample testing, demonstrating its efficiency and applicability.

2.3.2. Spiking neural network
SNN is a type of artificial neural network that emulate the brain's biological
decision-making
processes
through
spiking
neurons.
In
the
brain,
neurons
communicate by propagating electrical signals. They accumulate membrane potentials
by integrating incoming spike potentials. When the membrane potential exceeds a
certain threshold, the neuron generates a new spike potential that is transmitted to the
next neuron.Fig.1 illustrates the process of classifying Surface-Enhanced Raman
Scattering (SERS) spectra using an SNN. First, spike trains are generated through
spike encoding based on the provided SERS spectra, resulting in a spike matrix. This
spike matrix serves as the input to the SNN. Neurons within the SNN compute their
membrane potentials using the input spike matrix. When a neuron's membrane
potential surpasses a predefined threshold, it emits a new spike signal. Through this
propagation mechanism, the model classifies the SERS spectra by analyzing the firing
frequency of the spikes.

3.2.
Experiments of the Algorithm
Quantitative analysis has garnered significant research interest in the field of
SERS. Due to the nonlinear relationship between SERS spectra and substance
concentration, most studies modify the original SERS data to satisfy the modelŌĆÖs
assumptions, which often results in the loss of some characteristic information. In this
context, SNN are employed as prediction models for SERS. This approach utilizes
pulse encoding to capture the characteristic information of SERS spectra and
leverages LIF neurons for concentration prediction and classification.

3.5.
Application of SNN in Real Samples
The SNN model was employed for the quantitative analysis of SERS spectra of
MG and thiram in real samples. As broad-spectrum fungicides, MG and Thiram have
been widely applied in marine aquaculture and agricultural production respectively,
serving
to
inhibit
the
excessive
proliferation
of
harmful
pathogens
and
microorganisms. However, the extensive use of MG and Thiram often leads to their
excessive residues in fish and agricultural products respectively, posing potential risks
to food safety and public health. It is essential to quantitatively detect their
concentrations in real samples. The SERS technique was employed to detect the
concentration of MG in real lake water samples and the concentration of Thiram on
the surface of real apple peels. Additionally, an accurate quantitative model, the SNN,
was developed to analyze the SERS spectra and evaluate the levels of MG and Thiram
in the respective samples. Lake water samples were collected from a local lake, and
apple samples were purchased from local supermarkets. Then, 0.0046 g of MG was
dissolved in the lake water to prepare a stock solution with a concentration of 0.01
mol/L. This stock solution was then serially diluted with lake water to obtain MG
solutions at concentrations of 100 ppm, 10 ppm, and 1 ppm. For each concentration, 2
╬╝L of the solution was deposited onto a commercial silica gel plate, followed by the
addition of 2 ╬╝L of AgNPs for SERS spectral acquisition, as illustrated in the

=== PAGE 15 ===
Fig.7(a).Then, 0.026 g of thiram was dissolved in ethanol to prepare a stock solution
with a concentration of 0.01 mol/L. This stock solution was then diluted to obtain
thiram solutions at concentrations of 1000 ppm, 100 ppm, and 10 ppm. For each
concentration, 2 ╬╝L of the solution was sprayed onto the surface of cleaned apples,
followed by the addition of 2 ╬╝L of AgNPs for SERS spectral acquisition, as
illustrated in the Fig.7(c).The SERS spectral data were processed using SNN, CNN,
and MLP models, respectively. The predicted concentrations of MG and thiram
obtained from these models in real samples are shown in Fig.7(b,d). It can be
observed that the fitting curve of the SNN model is closest to the original curve.
Specifically, its R┬▓ value for MG reaches 0.96, outperforming those of the CNN and
MLP models (0.92 and 0.86, respectively). For thiram, the R┬▓ value of the SNN model
is 0.95, which is higher than the 0.91 of the CNN model and 0.83 of the MLP model.

3.6.
Conclusion
In this study, we developed a novel neurodynamics-based artificial intelligence

=== PAGE 16 ===
model employing a SNN for quantitative SERS spectral detection. The model
incorporates a spike encoding layer with LIF neurons to rapidly and accurately
analyze spectral features, enabling high-precision predictive capabilities. These
mehod was validated by using pesticides in real sample, the model demonstrates
robust extraction of authentic SERS features and a strong correlation between
concentration and prediction. The experimental results indicate that the SNN
outperforms CNN and MLP, achieving higher R┬▓ values and lower MAE and MSE,
reflecting predictions closer to true values.These findings position the SNN as a more
precise, convenient, and innovative approach for SERS spectral analysis and
quantitative detection.
